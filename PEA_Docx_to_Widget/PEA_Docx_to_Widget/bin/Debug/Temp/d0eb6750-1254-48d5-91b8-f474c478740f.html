Text_Text 

In your life you can group your daily experiences into ‘things you can’t touch or see’, like your thoughts and feelings, and ‘things you can touch and see’, like your school desk and trees. These things you can touch and see is called ‘matter’. You can touch, see and observe matter in a range of ways. Matter is the stuff that is around us and makes up our physical bodies.

In this lesson you will learn what matter is, and how we observe it in the forms of solids, liquids or gases.

Title Common misconceptions
Main-text Text_Heading
Text_Text
Misconception Remediation
All solids have regular (square or rectangular) shapes. This misconception may come from particle diagrams often representing solids in an overall regular shape. Students should be shown examples of hard solids (e.g. crystal or block of wood) and soft solids (e.g. fabric or foam ball). Particle diagrams of solids that have a regular internal structure but an overall irregular shape should also be introduced to students.

Title  Differentiation
Main-text   

Tab 1 

  

 Tab_Heading   Content
Tab_Reveal-text 

The concept of ‘matter’ is a challenging one for many students as it can be seen as rather abstract. By focussing on solids, liquids and gases (terms that students are usually familiar with) the idea that ‘stuff’ can be in many different forms and that solids, liquids and gases are different forms of matter is developed.

The spark activity encourages students to consider how the properties of substances are described. At this stage it is good to focus on the observable, and let students become familiar with the terms used to describe the properties, such as flow, shape and boundaries.

Changes of state and particles are considered later in the topic. In Lessons 7–14 the concept that matter is stuff, which can either be solid, liquid or gas can be developed.

Students should be encouraged to consider the substances that objects are made from, rather than the focus being on the object. For example, the metal is solid, not the knife is solid. Showing students examples of different objects made of the same material, or the same objects made of different materials, can emphasise this learning point.
Tab 2  Tab_Heading  Process
Tab_Reveal-text  

Information about the properties of solids, liquids and gases is presented separately, including graphical examples. Students are then asked to summarise the states of matter and identify differences and similarities via digital questions.

An alternative entry point for students is to brainstorm what they know about solids, liquids and gases. This activity can be supported with physical examples of each state of matter for students to observe.

A survey of solids liquids and gases in a student’s home can be set as a homework activity, or a survey could be done around the classroom or school.

A hinge question can be developed around the most important properties used to differentiate between solids, liquids and gases.

An audit of solid and liquid waste and how it is treated and disposed of can be carried out which can lead to discussions around physical properties such as compressibility, mass and strength, as well as chemical behaviour such as combustibility and biodegradability (concepts covered in Year 8).
Tab 3  Tab_Heading  Product
Tab_Reveal-text  

At this stage student descriptions of the properties are more important than correct scientific (or chemical) names for the substances, but correct terms should be given when they arise to increase familiarity with correct scientific language.

As well as completing the digital questions, students can use a range of text types to describe the differences between solids, liquids and gases, including:

• diagrams with examples of solids, liquids and gases

• tables comparing key properties

• mini booklets created from folding paper

• listing key properties and/or examples on sticky notes.
Tab 4  Tab_Heading  Extension
Tab_Reveal-text  

There is an opportunity to investigate properties of substances that are intermediate between solid and liquid, and liquid and gas, and this can be helpful when considering arrangements and attractions of particles later in the topic.

Some students might like to investigate plasma (gas made up from charged particles) in more detail, although the formation of ions is not normally considered until Year 9 or 10 in the Australian Science curriculum.

There is no reason to avoid discussions around particles at an early stage in this topic, but teachers should be aware that students may have developed many pre-existing misconceptions about the nature and behaviour of particles on the molecular and atomic scale.

Tab_Reveal-text

Text_Text

Your body is made up of many different <gloss:substance|substances> that <gloss:function|function> to keep you healthy. Think of all the substances that are in or part of your body.

Draw a three-column table in your notebook with the headings ‘Substance’, ‘Function’ and ‘Properties’. Write down five substances that are in or part of your body in the ‘Substance’ column in your notebook.
Text_Heading What do the substances do?
Text_Text Think about what each of these substances does in your body. Do they have a special role or job to keep your body healthy? Write your ideas in the ‘Function’ column in your notebook.
Text_Heading What are the properties of the substances?
Text_Text Think about what each substance looks and feels like and how it behaves. These characteristics are the <gloss:properties|properties> of the substance. Do these properties help the substance to carry out its function? Write your ideas in the ‘Properties’ column in your notebook.
Text_Heading Solids, liquids and gases in your body
Text_Text Now that you understand more about each substance, classify each as a solid, liquid or gas. Use a different coloured pencil or symbol for solid, liquid and gas. Draw a key next to your table to show which colour or symbol you used for each.

TemplateID Text_001

Title
Main-text Text_Heading Is everything a solid, liquid or gas?
Text_Text

Think about all the things that you might use, eat and drink every day. Some of these things might be easy to identify as solid, liquid or gas, such as a plate (solid), water (liquid) and the air you breathe (gas).

However, some substances can be harder to identify as one of the three states of matter. For example, do you think bread, yoghurt, jelly and toothpaste are a solid, liquid or a gas? This might be because they are <gloss:mixture|mixtures> of more than one substance.
Text_Heading Plasma
Text_Text

There is also a fourth state of matter called plasma. Plasma usually exists at very high temperatures, for example in our Sun and other stars, and in lightning. We don’t see plasma too often, so this topic will focus on solids, liquids and gases.

<insert|image|PSC07_PAR_L01_006.jpg>

The aurora borealis (northern lights) are a form of plasma.
Text_Heading Mixtures
Text_Text

Some substances can be a mixture of solids, liquids and gases. For example, meringue is air (gas) trapped inside a cooked mixture of egg white (liquid) and sugar (solid). The shaving gel shown here is another example of a mixture.
