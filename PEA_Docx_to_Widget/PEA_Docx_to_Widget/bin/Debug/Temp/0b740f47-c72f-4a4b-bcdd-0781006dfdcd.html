Main-text Text_Heading Content
Text_Text

The process of freezing is introduced in Lesson 10 The particle theory describes freezing and condensation, where the focus is on the loss of energy of the particles. The Spark activity introduces a range of situations where freezing can occur. This activity can be used to demonstrate the idea that there are two factors that can influence the rate of freezing; the rate of the removal of heat (in this case placing the water in the freezer) and how the rate of cooling of the water is slowed by environmental factors such as the size and shape of the container, the amount of the water or other substances within the water.

Text_Heading Process
Text_Text

Students can be supported in writing their inquiry question by providing examples of good inquiry questions. At this stage, the terms ‘dependent variable’ and ‘independent variable’ do not need to be introduced. For example, ‘Will orange juice freeze more quickly that apple juice?’ is a perfectly valid question that can be investigated. <download|prac_1_lab_notes.pdf|Lab notes!>

A SkillBuilder [insert link to PSS78_GEN_SB_16_Investigable questions] for writing investigable questions is available for students to use as required, which is supported by the Worked example [insert link to PSS78_GEN_WE_16_Investigable questions]. These resources can help students develop an investigable question in an appropriate way.

Once the inquiry question has been decided on, the main support required for students is conducting the investigation in a way that will produce meaningful results. The set-up can be difficult, especially deciding on a way to test whether and when the liquids have frozen. Demonstrating techniques and giving students specific guidance can help here. You can also refer to the teacher notes throughout the investigation.
Text_Heading Product
Text_Text

A SkillBuilder [insert link to PSS78_GEN_SB_16_Investigable questions] for writing investigable questions is available for students to use as required, which is supported by a worked example [insert link to PSS78_GEN_WE_16_Investigable questions]. These can help students to present an investigable question in an appropriate way.

Students can demonstrate their skills and understanding during and after the inquiry activity in a variety of ways, including:

  • sharing their investigable question with other students to obtain feedback

  • outlining their proposed method with other students or the teacher

    • recording the results of the practical investigation in their notebook

    • completing the online questions and/or the questions on the downloadable worksheet

  • completing an investigation report

  • sharing their final results with other students verbally or via a presentation.
Text_Heading Environment
Text_Text

Due to the technical nature of the inquiry activity and the requirement to collect data over a long period of time, working in mixed ability groups may be beneficial.

Students could carry out the investigation at home, where they have access to a freezer over a longer period of time. In this case, students can be provided with templates for recording their results and guidance around checking for freezing at regular intervals over a period of time (for example every 10 minutes over a period of 60 minutes).
Text_Heading Extension
Text_Text

This investigation can be extended to the Year 7 topic Mixtures. For example, students can be asked ‘how do mixtures of different substances affect the attractions between the particles and how does this influence the freezing process?’

  1. Why do we freeze food?

    1. What type of foods do you freeze at home?

      1. Can you freeze any food? If not, why not?

  2. Does it matter how quickly we can freeze food?

  3. What is your favourite frozen food?

Students can consider more complex and mathematical treatments of the different shaped containers and the amounts of liquid used. For example, ‘will twice as much water (mass or volume) take twice as long to freeze?’ ‘Does it matter what shape container this water is put into?’

One interesting observation that can be made in this investigation is that hot water can freeze more quickly than cold water. This is because evaporation of liquid from the hot water reduces the volume of the water and the reduced volume does not take as long to freeze. Convection currents can also increase the rate of cooling in the hot water.

Main-text Text_Heading Introduction
Text_Text

<gloss:freezing|Freezing> occurs when particles in a liquid lose energy and their movement slows down. When the particles move less, they form a solid. The temperature at which a liquid becomes solid is called its <gloss:freezing point|freezing point>.

In this activity, the rate of freezing is the <gloss:dependent variable|dependent variable>. The <gloss: independent variable|independent variable> is something that changes the rate of freezing.

In this investigation you will be selecting an independent variable to investigate. You will write an inquiry question about whether changing this independent variable will affect how quickly a liquid freezes..
Text_Heading What makes a good inquiry question?
Text_Text A good inquiry question is one that can be answered by carrying out an investigation. These are called <gloss: investigable question|investigable questions>.
Text_Heading What are possible inquiry questions for this investigation?
Text_Text

The following are ideas for questions that you can use for this investigation. You can use one of these, change one of these or write your own question. <gloss:franchise|franchises>

  • Does cold water freeze faster than warm water?

  • Does the shape of the container affect how quickly the water will freeze?

  • Does the <gloss: volume|volume> of water affect how quickly the water will freeze?

  • Does saltwater freeze faster than freshwater?

  • Do different fruit juices freeze at different rates?

Main-text Text_Heading Design your investigation
Text_Text

Develop the method for your investigation using the guidelines below.

  1. Write your step-by-step method in your notebook.

  2. Plan what measurements or observations you will make and how you will record them.

  3. Include safety information, including any potential risks.
Text_Heading Hints
Text_Text

  • The steps in your method should be clear and easy to follow. A good test is to ask yourself “If another student read these steps, would they know what to do?”

  • Consider the variables that will not change (the <gloss: controlled variable|controlled variables>) throughout your investigation. Mention the controlled variables in your method and describe how you are going to keep them constant throughout the investigation.

  • • Once you have designed your method, check in with your teacher for feedback.

Text_Text Make sure that the only thing that is changing in your investigation is the independent variable that you have chosen to investigate. Text_Text Once you have completed the investigation, use the evidence from the investigation (your measurements and observations) to answer your inquiry question and write a conclusion. You may be asked to present a written or verbal report to justify your conclusions.

Title Teacher notes
Main-text Text_Heading Recording data
Text_Text

This investigation is a good opportunity for students to develop and test an inquiry question. The measurement of the dependent variable (i.e. the time it takes for water to freeze) is challenging, especially when access to a freezer is limited. For this reason, the practical part of the investigation could be carried out at home, or with the cooperation of the school’s food technology department or the school canteen.

Wherever the freezing part of the process takes place, the following tips may be useful:

  • Encourage students to keep the volume of liquids small (e.g. less than )

  • Use transparent containers if possible

  • Place an object, such as a toothpick, partway into the liquid to determine whether the liquid has frozen

  • Encourage the students to check the liquids every 10–15 minutes only as regularly opening the freezer can cause it to warm up.

A suitable structure for a results table is shown here:

Time in freezer (minutes)
Volume of water (mL) 0 10 20 30 40 50 60
10 liquid liquid liquid liquid solid liquid / solid solid
5 liquid liquid liquid liquid / solid liquid / solid solid solid

If logistical issues make it difficult for all students to conduct the investigation, it can be conducted as a class or assigned to one group to conduct and report findings to the class.
Text_Heading Evaluation
Text_Text The challenge of precisely measuring freezing time can lead to some valuable discussion around improving data collection techniques and the precision of results. Possible solutions include temperature probes and web cams for monitoring when the liquids freeze.
