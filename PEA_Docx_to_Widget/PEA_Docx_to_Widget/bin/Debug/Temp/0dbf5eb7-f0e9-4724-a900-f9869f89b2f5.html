[color(#000000)]Title[color] False-colour images
Main-text

[color(#000000)]Remotely sensed images are produced from data gathered by satellite-mounted sensors. These sensors are so sensitive that they can record the radiation given off by features on the Earth’s surface. These data are then converted into images. Often, these images are referred to as [color][color(#000000)]false-coloured [color][color(#000000)]images, and the observer needs to know what each colour represents in order to interpret the image.[color][color(#000000)] [color]

[color(#000000)]Table 1: Colour guide for false-coloured images[color]

Colour Feature
Dark blue–black Deep water in oceans, lakes and dams
Mauve–steely blue Urban and industrial areas
Blue–light blue Arid scrubland; very shallow water
Dark green Deep, muddy floodwaters; clear, shallow water
Light green Moist, ploughed, bare soils; light grass cover
Brown Drier vegetation such as eucalypts and arid woodlands; bare rock
Red Healthy growing vegetation; rainforest (deep red); growing crops and pastures; mangroves (deep red)
Pink–red Early growth of crops and grasslands; suburban gardens, lawns and parks
Yellow Areas with little vegetation cover; heavily grazed areas; deserts and sand dunes
White–cream Bare ground; dry sand and salt areas; dunes and beaches; clouds

[color(#000000)]Title[color] True-colour images
Main-text

[color(#000000)]True-colour[color][color(#000000)] images feature colours as they appear to the human eye.[color][color(#000000)] These images [color][color(#000000)]look more like photographs because computer programs convert the data received from satellites into [color][color(#000000)]true-colour [color][color(#000000)]images. As satellites became more sophisticated, they were able to capture the data necessary to produce [color][color(#000000)]true-colour [color][color(#000000)]images. We still, however, need to know what each colour represents. [color]

In Figure 5, a large area of land is coloured white–cream. Apart from the areas still covered in snow (white), the white-cream is indicative of land without vegetation—land covered by volcanic ash following the eruption.

[color(#000000)]Colour guide for true-coloured images[color]

Colour Feature
Dark blue–black Deep, clear water in oceans, lakes and dams
Light blue Shallow water
Mauve–steely blue Urban and industrial areas
Brown–light brown Dry vegetation such as eucalypts and arid woodlands; bare rock
Bright light green Grassland, growing crops and pastures; suburban parks and gardens
Bright green Healthy, growing green vegetation; rainforest and mangroves
Light pink–orange– brown Cleared farming land; early growth in crops and grasslands
White–cream Bare ground; dry sand and salt areas; dunes and beaches; clouds

Main-text [color(#000000)]Text_Heading[color] [color(#000000)]Steps to interpreting aerial photographs[color]
[color(#000000)]Text_Text[color]

  1. [color(#000000)]Determine whether the photograph is a <[color][color(#000000)]gloss[color][color(#000000)]:vertical[color][color(#000000)] aerial [color][color(#000000)]photograph|[color][color(#000000)]vertical[color][color(#000000)] aerial photograph>[color][color(#000000)] or an <[color][color(#000000)]gloss:oblique[color][color(#000000)] aerial [color][color(#000000)]photograph|[color][color(#000000)]oblique[color][color(#000000)] aerial photograph>[color][color(#000000)].[color]

  2. [color(#000000)]Look for evidence of location and time. Often the caption provides some relevant information about the photograph, especially about the location. [color]

  3. [color(#000000)]Look for a familiar feature of the built environment that will give you some indication of scale.[color][color(#000000)] [color]

  4. [color(#000000)]Identify the main features of the photograph. You may find it useful to group them under the following headings. [color]
    [color(#000000)]Features of the <[color][color(#000000)]gloss:biophysical[color][color(#000000)] [color][color(#000000)]environment|[color][color(#000000)]biophysical[color][color(#000000)] environment>[color][color(#000000)]: [color]

  • [color(#000000)]landforms; for example, relief and drainage features [color]

  • [color(#000000)]climate [color]

  • [color(#000000)]v[color][color(#000000)]egetation[color][color(#000000)].[color]
    [color(#000000)]Features[color][color(#000000)] of the built environment: [color]

  • [color(#000000)]land use[color]

  • [color(#000000)]transport networks[color]

  • [color(#000000)]settlements [color]

  • [color(#000000)]rural[color][color(#000000)] and urban. [color]

  1. Ask yourself the following questions:

  • [color(#000000)]Is the area predominantly characteristic of the physical and[color][color(#000000)] built environments?[color][color(#000000)] [color]

  • [color(#000000)]What is the physical nature of the environment: fluvial, coastal, arid, glacial, mountainous and so on? [color]

  • [color(#000000)]To what extent has the area been modified by human activity?[color]

Main-text

[color(#000000)]The [color][color(#202122)]National Aeronautics and Space Administration’s [color][color(#000000)]Earth Observatory ([color]https://earthobservatory.nasa.gov/[color(#000000)]) is a great source of satellite imagery.[color]

[color(#000000)]The Earth Observatory was established in 1999 and is the principal source of satellite imagery and other scientific information related to climate and the environment provided by NASA for public use.[color]

[color(#000000)]Another great source of imagery is the United [color][color(#000000)]States[color][color(#000000)] Geological Survey (USGS)[color][color(#000000)]: [color]https://www.usgs.gov/[color(#000000)].[color]

For Australian imagery[color(#000000)],[color][color(#000000)] go to Digital Earth Australia: [color]https://www.dea.ga.gov.au/[color(#000000)] [color][color(#000000)].[color]
